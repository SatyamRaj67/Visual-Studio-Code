Fire dependence applies to species of plants and animals that rely on the effects of fire to make the environment more hospitable for their regeneration, growth, and regrowth. Being fire-adapted means plants and animals have behaviors, physical features, and/or characteristics enabling them to live, survive, and sometimes thrive with repeated fire. Examples of fire-adapted species include ponderosa pine (Pinus ponderosa), western larch (Larix occidentalis), and snowbrush ceanothus (Ceanothus velutinus). When change occurs too fast or too intensively, organisms cannot adapt quickly enough and will leave the system, replaced by those organisms that can.

Core beliefs of the Science of Fire Ecology

  1. Fire is a critical ecological process in many ecosystems throughout the world.
  2. Land-management goals often reflect plant communities with a past history of repeated fire events. However, fire regimes have been significantly altered on many landscapes, which may threaten native plant and animal assemblages, resulting in uncharacteristic ecological consequences.
  3. Plant communities, species composition, and soils have been significantly altered on many landscapes, causing change in the fire regime.
  4. Cultural burning has historically been part of the fire regime in many areas of the world.
  5. Restoring and maintaining native plant and animal assemblages and appropriate fire regimes is desired, although it is recognized that this may not always be possible.
  6. Science and education are critical in helping us understand ecological patterns and processes, how land management has affected fire regimes, and how vegetation and fire regimes can be restored.
  7. Science should inform both policy and land-management decisions that affect fire regimes.

Plant succession

A fire starts when fuel, heat, and oxygen interact together. Remove any one of these and the fire goes out. he interaction between intensity, severity, and frequency of fire as a disturbance process and vegetation as fuel across a landscape creates different patterns in the successional process, structure, and composition of vegetation, to which some animal species have adapted. Vegetation structure and composition also effects how fire behaves. Successional patterns within and between associations of plants across a landscape also continue in the absence of major disturbances. Landscape patterns created by disturbance or by the absence of disturbance form habitat for opportunistic species.

The fire regime of an area is defined as the historical context for the pattern, frequency, and severity of wildland fire interacting with that area’s topography, weather, and vegetation.

Fire intensity (the amount of energy or heat given off by a forest fire at a specific point in time); severity; the effects of a fire on the environment, including vegetation and soils; and frequency will shape the structure and composition of vegetation across the landscape over time.

Fire Regime

Fire regime refers to the nature of fire occurring over long periods (generally hundreds of years) and to the prominent immediate effects of fire that generally characterize an ecosystem.

Fire regimes are patterns of wildland fires that include factors such as frequency, extent, intensity, severity, type, and reason for burning. The role fire plays in an ecosystem varies with the characteristics under which the ecosystem evolved and or adapted.

The interactions of humidity conditions, wind, temperature, fuels, and ignition sources will determine the fire regime for a particular land area. Both frequency and intensity of fire vary and are interdependent. Frequency of fire depends on the ignition sources and the duration and character of weather.

Intensity of fire depends on the quantity of fuel available and the fuels’ combustion rates. Wind, topography, and other weather influences, such as temperature and humidity, also affect the interaction between frequency and intensity of fires.

Fire Behavior across an Environmental

Many components contribute to a wildland fire behavior. One of these components is topography. Fire will generally burn uphill faster than downhill. The steeper the slope and the higher the fuel load, the faster the fire will burn, especially with wind driving the burning.

Fuel load and depth can help determine whether a fire ignites, its rate of spread, and its intensity. Fuel load and depth directly relate to vegetation structure and composition. Structure and composition are the result of vegetation adapted to slope, elevation, precipitation, and soil types—and to disturbance succession. As vegetation changes in response to the environmental gradient, fire behavior will also change.


Fire Effects to Abiotic

When wetlands catch fire, the flames aren't as fierce, giving animals and plants a better chance to recover. These areas become sanctuaries where wildlife can find safety from the harshness of fires. The fires that touch wetlands often create more open spaces and encourage new plant growth, offering fresh food for animals. Places like streams, lakes, and meadows can work like shields against fires, thanks to their moisture. Their humidity levels are usually higher than the surrounding dry lands, making it harder for fires to spread there. Additionally, wetlands being lower in elevation allows cooler air to settle, especially during evenings, making it less prone to intense fires. In the aftermath of a fire, the landscape doesn't look uniform. Instead, it resembles a patchwork quilt, with areas that burned fiercely, others that saw milder burns, and spots that escaped the flames. This mosaic pattern creates diverse habitats that support various animal needs like food, shelter, and breeding grounds. After a fire, the remnants like ash and charred materials can travel through the air and settle into water bodies. These materials end up at the bottom, forming a historical record of fire's intensity and impact over time. As a fire burns, the heat it generates changes the soil beneath. Sometimes, this heat is so intense that it alters the soil's physical and chemical makeup. Nutrients and chemicals can transform, affecting the land's future growth.


Fire Effects to Biotic

  1. Vegetation gradient
  2. Plants are incredible adapters, shaping themselves as individuals and communities to fit different environments. They take root in various habitats, from damp valleys to arid hilltops. Factors like weather (sunlight, rain, winds) and landscape features (slope, height, direction) also influence how plants evolve, leading to variations in their makeup, arrangement, and diversity across different settings. These elements also create unique types of fuel, influencing moisture levels, size, shape, continuity, and arrangement. Persistent or occasional disturbances experienced by plant communities, such as fires or other disruptions, further alter how fuels appear. Over time, plants have developed specific characteristics to cope with these disturbances. An everyday example is the USDA Plant Hardiness Zone Map, which helps gardeners choose plants suited for their climate. This map showcases plants' adaptations to environmental gradients. Unlike animals, plants can't move to new places when conditions change. Instead, they've evolved over time to respond to frequent fire occurrences. Trees like ponderosa pine and western larch are fire-resistant and sun-loving compared to shade-tolerant species like western redcedar and western hemlock. Ponderosa pine and giant sequoia have thick bark that resists fire, while lodgepole pine can thrive after intense fires due to special cones and partnerships with mycorrhizae, aiding them in colonizing poor nutrient areas. Snowbrush ceanothus, a sun-tolerant plant, has seeds with hard shells that need high-temperature fires to open and sprout. These seeds can lie dormant for centuries until a wildfire triggers them. This plant can fix nitrogen and vanish when overshadowed by trees. All in all, plants have finely tuned themselves to their environments, evolving unique traits and behaviors in response to persistent challenges like fires. This intricate adaptation has allowed them to survive, thrive, and play crucial roles in various ecosystems, contributing to the remarkable tapestry of life on Earth.
  3. Vegetation succession
  4. Plants exhibit adaptability to various factors such as precipitation levels, soil types, solar radiation variations (slope and aspect), and elevation, even when not subjected to disturbances. In the absence of disturbances, plants still adjust to factors like disturbance frequency, intensity, and severity. The impact of disturbances, determined by their severity and intensity, leads to immediate effects on species and communities, along with longer-lasting consequences. Even without disturbances, species and communities progress through succession, potentially resulting in densely overpopulated forests or grasslands. Ecosystems remain in a state of constant change, whether disturbances are present or absent. Plant communities, despite occasionally having successional stages that can endure for centuries, are not static. They can undergo shifts in structure, composition, and function in response to disturbances or their absence. Plants display opportunistic behavior to gain a competitive advantage and ensure the survival of populations. Illustrating fire-adapted traits, snowbrush ceanothus (Ceanothus velutinus) exemplifies this adaptability. This adaptation includes the production of seeds that remain dormant in the organic layer for hundreds of years until a very intense fire (exceeding 200°F) breaks down the seed coat, enabling germination. Thriving in open, full-sun conditions following a fire, ceanothus is highly intolerant to shade and possesses nitrogen-fixing roots, making it particularly suited for establishing after severe fires. Its shade-intolerant nature means it exits the ecosystem once shaded by tree species, returning only when another fire clears the area. The plant's leaves, twigs, and stems contain volatile waxes or oils, emitting a potent odor when crushed or exposed to intense sunlight. The gummy, resinous sap also emits a strong scent and is exceedingly flammable, facilitating the germination and growth of other seeds. In contrast, false huckleberry (Menziesia ferruginea) is an example of a species not adapted to fire. Unlike ceanothus, it displays high shade tolerance and thrives best in low light conditions (around 5% of full sunlight). Typically found in cool, moist environments on northerly slopes, false huckleberry possesses fire-resistant characteristics due to its moist, flexible leaves. It does not accumulate dry, dead material within its structure, and its sap lacks a distinctive odor. The aftermath of a fire depends on landscape features, fire severity, and species involved. Such events invariably initiate a series of changes as plants, microorganisms, fungi, and other organisms recolonize the burned area. As trees and plants mature, light availability and other factors shift, leading to changes in the composition of the local ecosystem.
  5. Wildlife
  6. Fires alter wildlife habitats, which, like forests, evolve and adapt to disturbances, as do other natural systems. Fires reshape the proportions, arrangements, and qualities of habitats across landscapes. Post-fire, there may be temporary changes in food and shelter availability. Animals, being opportunistic, may shift preferences, transitioning from dense forest to open, warm conditions. Some animals move from unsuitable burned areas to unburned ones. Adjacent unburned regions offer varied habitats, aiding wildlife with food and cover options. Herbivores and those seeking herbaceous cover favor post-burn grass/forb or broad-leafed forest habitats. Burning can enhance forage and nutritional content for wildlife for years to a century, varying with vegetation type. Fire-killed trees sustain insect larvae, then birds. Snags and debris are vital for cavity nesters, reptiles, small mammals, and even large ones like bears. Fire creates temporary habitat for some species. Cavity-nesting birds prefer high- to moderate-burn severity, open-cup ground nesters occupy moderate to low severity areas, while canopy-nesting species favor unburned zones. Fire-killed trees sustain insects and snags, serving as perches and feeding sites for birds. The black-backed woodpecker flourishes during insect outbreaks, its population paralleling insect numbers.
  7. Humans
  8. Humans are integral to the biosphere, interconnected with global ecosystems. We now contribute as significant disturbance agents. We can introduce vegetation to non-migratory areas and plant species not naturally adaptable. Attempts to control or fear fire have impacted us all. While we can't generate hurricanes or tornadoes, we control fire for beneficial or destructive ends. Over 90% effectiveness in wildland fire suppression doesn't prevent thousands of yearly acres burning, the elusive 10%. “Five out of six U.S. wildfires in the last two decades have human origins, extending wildfire seasons and altering their timing," a 2017 study states. People-sparked fires triple the season length, making East start earlier and West last longer (Balch, et al. 2017). The 1998 Florida fires were record-setting, triggered by human negligence. Historical coastal prairie fire—nature's mechanism—controlled grasses and trees. Fire suppression, grazing, mowing, and urban sprawl curtailed its role. Failing to manage fuel accumulation led to intensified 1998 and 2007 fires. We created tinderboxes by neglecting fire ecology, favoring thinning to prevent conflagrations. Understanding fire ecology benefits us. Applying time-tested ecosystem concepts helps us coexist. Choosing where to grow a tree or build influences outcomes. Managed fire-adapted systems yield benefits:
  1. Fire clears underbrush, brightening the forest floor, nurturing soil, bolstering established trees.
  2. Wildlands thrive post-fire, attracting diverse wildlife with new growth.
  3. Fire eliminates pests, enriches soil, with more trees lost to insects and disease than fire.
